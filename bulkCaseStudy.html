Bulk Spice Identifier Case Study

View Wireframe

Challenge

I noticed that many of my friends, neighbors, and family shop for bulk spices at the local co-op grocery stores in Minneapolis as a way to save money, select exactly how much quantity of a certain spice they want, and to reduce packaging waste. When shopping in the bulk aisle, customers have to scoop out their spice and write the PLU number on the container. They are not required to write down the name of the spice. One day, as I was baking a cake with a friend, my friend went to the cupboard to find the cornstarch, and she saw nothing labeled “cornstarch,” just a few co-op containers with PLU numbers and no names. My friend thought that she had purchased cornstarch at one time, but had no idea what any of the PLU numbers before her meant. I watched as her friend threw away three containers of unnamed bulk spices because she did not know what they were, and then headed out the door to pick up some cornstarch from the grocery store just so we could finish our recipe. This inspired me to create something that would help people identify their spices bought from co-op grocery stores so that they don’t waste time and waste spices.


Opportunity

I thought that creating an application to discover the name of spices from co-op grocery stores would help in saving time and reducing waste. However, I was not sure how prevalent this problem was and among who, so I decided to interview co-op shoppers.

My Role: UX Wireframing, User Research, Mobile Design, Front and Back-end Development

Time Constraint: 15 days

Process

I wanted to understand if the problem of needing to identify spices bought from co-op grocery stores was a common issue, and if so, what would help customers to not waste time and money by buying new spices and throwing away unidentified bulk spices.

Research

I interviewed 10 co-op bulk aisle shoppers, and asked them questions about their age, job titles, reason for shopping in the bulk aisle at co-ops, and about their experience with bulk spices from co-ops. I found that overwhelmingly people did tend to forget to write the name of their bulk items down. I found that in almost every case of an interviwee finding an unnamed bulk spice in their cupboard, they had to go buy a new one. They would first try to smell or taste the spice, but if they could not identify it that way, they had to buy a new one. I also found that in many instances users were in their kitchen cooking or baking something when they found their unnamed spice.

User Profile

After conducting the user interviews, I analyzed the results and came up with our user profile. Name: Matt Hansen
Young: Age 21-30
Family: Single
Occupation: Program Coordinator
Technology: iPhone
Architype: Buys bulk for environmental and financial reasons. Is busy and grocery shops for just himself and without a list.
He wants to shop at the co-op, but likes to do it as cheaply as possibly.

Analysis and Process

Based on my user interviews and resulting user profile, I designed for simplicity and ease of use. I wanted to create an application that was easy to use and designed for when users are in the kitchen cooking.

Development

I whiteboarded out my database design as well as all of the functionality of the pages. I designed the application to be mobile first and responsive. I wanted the application to be simple and not involve a lot of clicking and any extra functionality since users would likely be already in the middle of cooking, and not wanting to spend too much time messing around on their phones. I used javascript, express, node.js, and angular to create my pages and views. The first main page is the log in page, and then the home page shows the co-ops, and the users can click on their co-op. The information on spices is populated via an angular ng-view partial. The log in feature was an important and challenging part of the development phase. Using passport.js, I developed the site to have log in capabilities. The database was developed using Postgresql and one of the biggest challenges was designing tables that were easily joined, as well as creating my own API of the local co-op grocery stores spices.
